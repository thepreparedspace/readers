"u" — which sound

The u_consonant_e pattern makes two unrelated sounds in English — a "yoo" glide (cute, mule, cube) and a plain "oo" sound (June, rule, flute) — with no spelling clue which applies. Choose which family the child works with, or show both as two separate tabs. Nuance worth knowing: the "oo" heard here is always the long oo — the same sound as "food" or "moon" in the oo-reader, not the short "look/book" sound. There is no short-oo version of this pattern, so this tab and "long oo" mean the same thing.

a note on "e_e"

This reader doesn't include an e-consonant-e tab. Checked carefully, almost no single-syllable e_e words in English stay fully clean — the handful that exist fail on a soft c or g (scene, gene), an "s" that quietly says /z/ (these), an r-controlled vowel (here, where), or are proper names (Eve, Pete). Rather than force in a weak word, this family is left out for now.

how to present this work

Tap the small name-slip at the top of each section first — the child hears the vowel's long sound alone. Each word card shows the whole word immediately: the vowel and the final "e" are both shown in red, since they work together as a pair even though a consonant sits between them. The consonant itself stays black — it keeps its own plain sound. Tapping any single letter, or either red letter, plays a real recorded human voice — not synthesized speech. Tapping the word itself reveals a speaker icon — tapping that speaks the whole word naturally, as a control of error the child can use independently.

keeping it phonetic

Every word here is fully decodable using sounds the child already has. Words with a soft c or g (race, cage), an "s" saying /z/ (rose, wise), an untaught digraph (white), or a genuine sight-word exception (have, come) have all been left out.

extending to the home

This reader is meant as an extension of the prepared environment into the home — usable by a parent unfamiliar with Montessori method, so the child can keep practicing independently between sessions.
